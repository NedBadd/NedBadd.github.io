Peugeot 205 GTi Engine Rebuild

November · 2025

Overview

This is a classic example of being backed into a corner by bad decisions, and using my education and free will to get me out of that corner. I bought this car and had already sunken significant effort into rebuilding components (sunroof, complete rear suspension rebuild, braking system etc). Falling for the sunken cost fallacy, when I went to top up the oil and was greeted with a milkshake under the oil cap, I knew I was in for a good time. If laying on your back in oily clothes is your idea of a good time.

The Problem

The 205 GTi has an all aluminium engine, with removeable cast iron cylinder liners. It is well documented that early versions of this engine suffer from water jacket corrosion if owners do not use coolant of the correct spec and/ or the car sits around not being used for a long time. Unfortunately it is also well documented that the engine block alloy is very difficult to weld to. This means the general advice is once your engine has suffered this corrosion, you should take it out, and find a better one. Two problems:

  • I didn't want to do that.
  • 205 GTi engines aren't exactly easy to come by in Australia. There were only around 2000 Peugeot 205s sold here.

What caused the mixture of oil and water?

At first I thought the problem was with the headgasket, and after a freshen up in the cooling jacket my water and oil would no longer be hanging out in the sump together. Unfortunately, it wasn't this straight forward, and the headgasket being so corroded it was in two pieces was simply a symptom of a deeper issue.

How I fixed it

This seemed bad. I had expert advice to give up on this block. But I had a few advantages:

  • Fundamentally, thanks to the removable liner design of this engine, the block is not stressed like it is in a more traditional 1-piece cast block. This material only exists to act as the water seal. The liner is the component that must handle cylinder pressure.
  • I have a 3d printer and experience with reinforced epoxy, with a will to use both of them.

The ordinary rebuild

Now I had a fighting chance, I got to rebuild the other things that it would be silly not to while the entire engine was apart.

Going the extra kilometer

The car started and ran. It took some massaging; the timing was way off at first so it barely accelerated, and it took me a while to figure out how to set base timing since 205 GTi's have many different Bosch ECUs depending on build year. The exhaust manifold to downpipe connection was leaking horribly, that was tricky because the geometry of the cast manifold meant no socket/ spanner I owned would fit in there. I had to find a cheap socket and cut into it with the grinder to overcome that.

My car had arrived in my life without an intake snorkel. I borrowed a snorkel off another car and decided to see if ASA-CF filament would survive when placed immediately next to the radiator. The answer is emphatically yes. I think my snorkel is arguably better than the standard one (the internal diameter is slightly larger, and it has a smoother transition).

Self belief, bordering on delusion

"Well, if I just rebuilt the entire car, what else could possibly go wrong?"" I thought smugly, feeling the unbridaled rush of having just rebuilt a car in my backyard with only a 3d printer and pure determination.
I tempted fate, but this time I got away with it. The car did a +3000km road trip to Newcastle and back without missing a beat. No milkshakes in the oil.
