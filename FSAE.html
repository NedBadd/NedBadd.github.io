Formula Student, my time as Head of Aerodynamics

Aerodynamic/ Mechanical Design and Manufacturing · 2019-2022

Background on Formula Student

Formula SAE or Formula Student is a competition where (usually engineering) students from universities all around the world compete against one another to design, build, test and race a custom, open wheeled formula style race car. The competition features no wheel-to-wheel racing (for safety reasons) and is instead scored based on teams' and cars' performance across a range of events, spanning static, and dynamic events.

The competition was intended to reward teams for following good engineering practise, making decisions based on data, and fully understanding the technology on their cars.


The Big FSAE Lesson

I would argue that the biggest lesson in Formula Student is this: don't get bogged down in detailed design - you have to stay conscious of the bigger picture at all times. It's exceedingly easy as an inexperienced university student to want to go off and prove to yourself (and everyone in your team) that you are competent in the theory, and the tools you've just been taught.

FSAE is the place where you realise that the real world is a different place to your exercise book, or CAD/ CAE. If you get lost in details, your timelines will slip, you and your team will have less time to fix all the things which will inevitably go wrong in early testing. Remember this car is custom made from the ground up by newly educated engineers. If your car doesn't work it's not going to be very fast.

The wise teams will be EXTREMELY cautious to not be overly ambitious with their design. Go for the lowest hanging fruit.

What you (an FSAE team member) get

At RMIT (at least while I was a student there) there was little to no academic or really any tangible benefit for your thousands of hours of dedication. Students were volunteers and gained nothing but experience for their trouble.

I'd argue however that this experience was the best of my engineering career so far. Total design freedom (at least within the rules) and a team of likeminded, motivated, and smart people to work with. Working on an FSAE team is like working at a startup, working on cutting edge technology. Communication is critical, you learn so much more than in classes.

It will be hard for me to do justice for the work I did in FSAE since I spent definitely thousands of hours spanning more than three years on RMIT's Electric Racing team (now called RMIT Motorsport).

First season, 2019

I joined FSAE in my fourth year at uni. When I joined, I was lucky to be able to demonstrate I was serious about getting stuck into the project early, and was invited to help on a lot of manufacturing. In 2019, RMIT Electric Racing was building the previous year's design, with the intent of ironing out the problems from the first build, and getting significant testing time.

I was lucky to join at this moment in time, as the 2019 team was exceptionally competent and organised.

Thanks to the realistic nature of the goals, and the fundamental strengths the R19e package, our car was competing at the top. I contributed heavily to aerodynamic manufacturing and learnt a lot about the team dynamics, composite manufacturing, hitting timelines, and pulling all nighters before deadlines with the team.

Some nerdy specs of R19e off the top of my head:

  • Drivetrain: Single rear motor, EMRAX228 - Chain drive through Drexler two-way LSD.
  • Vehicle Dynamics: Double unequal length carbon fibre a-arms, pushrod actuated fully adjustable springs and dampers
  • Chassis: Full composite monocoque, laser cut aluminium mould, a technique invented by RMIT Electric Racing saving significant time and money vs a traditional plug and mould technique.
  • Aerodynamics: Full aero package developed cohesively, extra focus placed on diffuser and underbody. Enclosed sidepods for cooling efficiency. Coefficent of lift by unit area (ClA) roughly -1.8.
  • ECU: Custom hardware and firmware developed by RMIT Electric Racing students
  • Max lateral acceleration: 2.1g
  • Weight: 218kg
  • 0-100km/h: 3.5s
  • Power: Limited by competition rules at 80kw.
Our car was arguably the best performing at the competition but we didn't score as well as was deserved thanks to a few unfortunate events.

2020 Season

2020 started with our clean sweep of Formula Student Sydney. We won every dynamic event and therefore won the EV class outright. Our car was reliable and fast, and our small team now knew how to run it. It softened the blow of losing the position we felt the car/ team deserved for the earlier competition.

COVID obviously threw a spanner in the works, but it afforded me the opportunity a lot of time sat at my computer to learn ANSYS FLUENT and the CFD process in general. I spent months staring at FLUENT mesh errors.

I learnt CFD without instruction from anyone on the team, as my predecessor had moved on from the team, and left minimal documentation. That made progress slow, but looking back I feel I really understand the process more deeply for having had to learn this way. As before, the best way to acquire a new skill is to get yourself in so deep that you have no option but to figure it out.

I just want to paint a picture of the state of CFD simulations at this point:

  • Simulations done manually, no automation at all. Every new piece of geometry must be meshed, solved, and post processed manually. It took at least a day of focused, deliberate work to complete one simulation, if nothing went wrong.
  • Computational resources were more or less non-existent. There were some murmurs that RMIT had gifted some old servers to the team, but nobody had any details and they weren't available for use. I used my laptop to solve early simulations. Because the computing power was so limited, these were almost certainly all wrong.
