Formula Student, my time as Head of Aerodynamics

Aerodynamic/ Mechanical Design and Manufacturing · 2019-2022

A large amount of content warning

FSAE was my obsession for three years. I learnt so much and feel so passionate about my time on the FSAE team at RMIT. This section is probably going to be unnecessarily long, wordy and borderline lectuer-ey. I've tried my hardest to keep it short but I didn't succeed.

Background on Formula Student

Formula SAE or Formula Student is a competition where (usually engineering) students from universities all around the world compete against one another to design, build, test and race a custom, open wheeled formula style race car. The competition features no wheel-to-wheel racing (for safety reasons) and is instead scored based on teams' and cars' performance across a range of events, spanning static, and dynamic events.

The competition was intended to reward teams for following good engineering practise, making decisions based on data, and fully understanding the technology on their cars.


The Big FSAE Lesson

I would argue that the biggest lesson in Formula Student is this: don't get bogged down in detailed design - you have to stay conscious of the bigger picture at all times. It's exceedingly easy as an inexperienced university student to want to go off and prove to yourself (and everyone in your team) that you are competent in the theory, and the tools you've just been taught in class.

FSAE is the place where you realise that the real world is a different place to your exercise book, or CAD/ CAE. If you get lost in details, your timelines will slip, you and your team will have less time to fix all the things which will inevitably go wrong in early testing. Remember this car is custom made from the ground up by newly educated engineers. If your car doesn't work it's not going to be very fast.

The wise teams will be EXTREMELY cautious to not be overly ambitious with their design. Go for the lowest hanging fruit.

What you (an FSAE team member) get

At RMIT (at least while I was a student there) there was little to no academic or really any tangible benefit for your thousands of hours of dedication. Students were volunteers and gained nothing but experience for their trouble.

I'd argue however that this experience was the best of my engineering career so far. Total design freedom (at least within the rules) and a team of likeminded, motivated, and smart people to work with. Working on an FSAE team is like working at a startup, working on cutting edge technology. Communication is critical, and you learn so much more than in classes.

It will be hard for me to do justice for the work I did in FSAE since I spent definitely thousands of hours spanning more than three years on RMIT's Electric Racing team (now called RMIT Motorsport).

First season, 2019

I joined FSAE in my fourth year at uni. When I joined, I was lucky to be able to demonstrate I was serious about getting stuck into the project early, and was invited to help on a lot of manufacturing. In 2019, RMIT Electric Racing was building the previous year's design, with the intent of ironing out the problems from the first build, and getting significant testing time.

I was lucky to join at this moment in time, as the 2019 team was exceptionally competent and organised. I was taught how to wet lay composite parts, and work with prepregs, molds etc by these exceptional team members, for that I was very lucky.

Thanks to the realistic nature of the goals, and the fundamental strengths the R19e package, our car was competing at the top. I contributed heavily to aerodynamic manufacturing and learnt a lot about the team dynamics, composite manufacturing, hitting timelines, and pulling all nighters before deadlines with the team.

Some nerdy specs of R19e off the top of my head:

  • Drivetrain: Single rear motor, EMRAX228 - Chain drive through Drexler two-way LSD.
  • Vehicle Dynamics: Double unequal length carbon fibre a-arms, pushrod actuated adjustable springs and dampers
  • Chassis: Full composite monocoque, laser cut aluminium mould, a technique invented by RMIT Electric Racing saving significant time and money vs a traditional plug and mould technique.
  • Aerodynamics: Full aero package developed cohesively, extra focus placed on diffuser and underbody. Enclosed sidepods for cooling efficiency. Coefficent of lift by unit area (ClA) roughly -1.8.
  • ECU: Custom hardware and firmware developed by RMIT Electric Racing students
  • Max lateral acceleration: 2.1g
  • Weight: 218kg
  • 0-100km/h: 3.5s
  • Power: Limited by competition rules at 80kw. The team believed the car could make 100kw comfortably. I later went on to drive this car a fair bit, and if commanded 100% throttle at almost any speed there was enough torque to light up the rear wheels, despite them being big, soft slicks.
Our car was arguably the best performing at the competition but we didn't score as well as was deserved thanks to a few unfortunate events.

2020 Season

2020 started with our clean sweep of Formula Student Sydney. Having proven myself to the established team through 2019, I was invited to be one of only ~10 people who ran the car in Sydney. We won every dynamic event and therefore won the EV class outright. Our car was reliable and fast, and our small team now knew how to run it. It softened the blow of losing the position we felt the car/ team deserved for the earlier competition.

COVID obviously threw a spanner in the works, but it afforded me the opportunity a lot of time sat at my computer to learn ANSYS FLUENT and the CFD process in general. I spent months staring at FLUENT mesh errors.

I learnt CFD without instruction from anyone on the team, as my predecessor had moved on from the team, and left minimal documentation. That made progress slow, but looking back I feel I really understand the process more deeply for having had to learn this way. As before, the best way to acquire a new skill is to get yourself in so deep that you have no option but to figure it out.

I just want to paint a picture of the state of CFD simulations at this point:

  • Simulations done manually, no automation at all. Every new piece of geometry must be meshed, solved, and post processed manually. It took at least a day of focused, deliberate work to complete one simulation, if nothing went wrong.
  • Computational resources were more or less non-existent. There were some murmurs that RMIT had gifted some old servers to the team, but nobody had any details and they weren't available for use. I used my laptop to solve early simulations. Because the computing power was so limited, these were almost certainly all wrong.
Something I will say in my defence of what you're about to see is, I was at least methodical about starting with simple simulations and slowly building up complexity more and more as I better understood the process.

R21e Design work

The team had plans for significant packaging changes for the following iteration of car. Most notably was the shift from the well developed single motor to individual hub motors with planetary gears. This meant the whole mechanical packaging concept (and hence aero) was due to change significantly.

At this stage, there wasn't a great deal of experience in the team regarding aerodynamic design, however myself and another team member tackled that together. Over the course of 2020, we developed our skills in CFD, bounced ideas around, and developed a totally new aerodynamics package. I'm skipping over months of CFD mesh errors, figuring out post processing, beginning to automate that, weekly meetings, discussions around design for manufacture etc.

Between myself and the other designer, we did ~200 3d CFD simulations mostly completely manually. We learnt a lot about how flow should be developed along an open wheeled race car.

The front wing was where I spent most of my effort. I designed, placed and tuned the so called side wings, and tuned the slot gaps for the second and third elements of the undertray, and designed the new rear wing endplates, while the rear wing elements were carried over since we had a well functioning DRS mechanism for that design. My partner designed the undertray, and diffuser, as well as all the mounting hardware and integration to the chassis to go along with those components.

R21e Build

I was personally and wholly responsible for building the entire R21/R22e Aero kit. Everything you see here is almost entirely my own work, except where I had help from junior team members.

Inspired by some other teams manufacturing techniques, my aero partner had conceptualised a new way to layup wings, with me contributing some details of execution. In 2019, our wings were made with ribs and spars, with the ribs providing the profile, a molded "d-nose" for the leading edge, with cured flat sheets of carbon forming the skin. This took a lot of work, as well as many cures to achieve. The new technique was a one cure per wing strategy.

Our design meant we had a "universal flap" which allowed one mold to be used repeatedly to cure wings for the front wing, upper side wings, and side diffusers. It's worth noting by this point my aero partner who had been helping graduated from his degree program and officially left the team. I was the head of the Aerodynamics subsystem and wholly responsible for this build and co-ordinating all elements.

Since the wings were a single cure, the part count dropped dramatically, but further, because the skin was all one piece, most wings could be left hollow as there was enough stiffness from two plys, and the bonded trailing edge.

We had no experience with aerofoil plug molds, and we wanted to keep costs low. My idea was to use MDF like we did on the R19e wet lay parts. I tested some sample MDF in the oven at ~100deg, which was the cure temperature of our prepreg resin.

The world was moving away from 3-axis CNC cut MDF molds, so we had to adapt and join the future where apparently people use foam instead. The molds for our wet lay and plug and mold parts were made using 3-axis CNC foam, followed up by a lot of filler, flowcoat and sanding. I made all but one of these molds on my own except one which I delegated to a junior member so he would have agency over when he wanted to come in and work on it, ownership, and get to do the entire process on his own without me.

CFD Automation FROM HERE ONWARDS IS STILL UNDER CONTRUCTION

I had never attempted anything really seriously computationally intensive, nor ever tried to script anything before. I could see how much time it would save me personally, but more importantly, I knew that this was critical in ensuring that our team could compete in development rate. The car I might design after all this work is only a reflection on the strengths of the team who build it. Racing is really just a development race between groups of engineers, with added chaos sometimes, but that's generally what it is.

FLUENT TEXT USER INTERFACE was the order of the year. I found a basic FSAE TUI journal file in an online youtube video. It was basic, but it had mesh improvement, definition of rotating wheels, unique mesh settings for a front wing, forward part of chassis, wheels and thats about it. It was a modest starting point.

At this point I was still working in named selections, where you use the names of your imported CAD bodies to set your mesh controls. Further, there were intermediate steps between CAD (Solidworks) and CFD, where you manually create the bodies of influence like wake mesh volumes etc.

Final year project/ Capstone Project

I chose to use my Capstone project to double down on FSAE design work. I felt I had progressed a long way since I did the design for the R22e front wing and side aero. I was very interested in the fundamentals aerodynamic design and knew that just performing the manufacturing duties for the aero kit I had already done wouldn't be satisfying. I had more ideas I needed to try, and felt I would be working on improving my scripting further regardless of if it counted for marks or not.

Big takeaways regarding FSAE Aerodynamic Design

  • It was absolutely worthwhile investing the many many days developing my scripting. I could achieve in an hour what used to take weeks. As the architect of the entire system, I knew exactly what to do if any step in the process were to fail. I also had a serious legacy to leave newer members on the team, who weren't privvy to how much suffering my code would save them.
  • Open wheel aerodynamics is all about wake management. FSAE is unique in the operating conditions our aerodynamic components must operate in are extreme. High yaw, slow speed.
