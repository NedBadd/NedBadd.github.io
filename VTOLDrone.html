VTOL 3d Printed Flying Wing

Hardware · 2026

Overview

As said in the intro, VTOL RPAS Drones seem a bit unelegant to me in their concept. This layout may not offer much tangible benefit, but doing something a bit strange is part of the fun. The idea is to make a folding airframe that is simply an X quad in VTOL mode, and folds away two of the four motors for more efficient forward flight. These motors could in theory be unfolded for a burst of power in cruise, but a flying wing was chosen for (hopefully) an increase in efficiency, and therefore range and endurance.

Hobby racing drone electronics are used, and since acquiring a 3d printer I knew I had to try 3d printing an airframe. Bambu sell a filament called "PLA-Aero" which is advertised as ideal for printing RC aircraft so I knew I had to give that a go.

Some background

I have been flying drones (or RC Planes as they were known back in the mid 2000's) since I was a child. I have always loved aircraft and flying. During my time at uni an assignment required us to build a plane. I had seen people online hot wire cutting their wings and even though it was going to take longer and weigh more than a foamboard plain wing, I wanted to do it anyway. I made a hot wire cutter at home using some bits of scrap wood and a guitar string. Insulation foam from bunnings was used as stock I made aerofoil templates from bits of plywood cut out against a printed scale paper version.

The first attempt at the folding drone

The idea for the folding drone is quite old. At first, the idea was to have the airframe/ wing split in half along its entire length, at about the point of max thickness. I built an almost functional version of this design some years ago, before ardupilot had tail sitting capabilities supported. Looking at videos online back then, everyone thought the "KK2" flight controller was the way to go for VTOL planes, so I got one of those. Even then, it was acceptionally outdated, with very limited documentation.

The modern day

The mk1 version was informative, even though I never attempted to fly it. The mode transition was all programmed in my transmitter, and I thought I might get away with only having any control input from the flight controller. Now I own a 3d printer I knew I had to leave the hotwire cutter in 2017 and try out the aforementioned PLA-Aero. PLA-Aero is a lightweight PLA which expands in the nozzle, lowering the overall density of the printed part at the expense of strength. Being 3d printed though, geometry can be tailored to offset the strength lost, and the lower weight is a worthwhile trade.

The challenges of PLA-Aero

PLA-Aero is, as mentioned, an active foaming filament. Foaming additives in the filament begin to sublimate when the filament is heated/ extruded. Because this reaction is taking place in the nozzle, you can't simply load your filament and press print like you usually can with Bambu printers. Usually FDM printing allows for accurate extrusion by simply retracting the filament feed from the nozzle as the exruder head travels from one position in space in the build volume to another. This means any geometry can be sliced easily since the printer is free to move from any position to another to continue the print.

You can't do that with foaming filament. Any print has to be completed in a continuous line, from start to finish. This inherently limits the geometry you can print, and so this should really be considered from the design stage. I can't unfortunately, as much as I wish I could, claim this design approach to be original. Tom Stanton explains this probably better than I just did here. I may have shamelessly copied the method, but there was enough work in just replicating this since:

  • His design is a plain wing, no taper, sweep, or twist. My flying wing has all three.
  • The internal structure needs to be created in CAD in such a way that the slicer not only CAN interpret it as a single continuous line, but DOES. Despite the Bambu slicer featuring "Spiral Vase Mode" this is still easier said than done.
Parametric CAD like Onshape is not really intended nor built for even model aircraft structures featuring every detail of a surface like this. I sunk lots of time and effort into creating a model that was workable yet still featured the detail I needed.

Before we even get to the trying to fly the plane

Leveraging my past experience, I feel it would be unwise to have my first experience with ardupilot be the moment I try to maiden this 3d printed drone. It would be good to verify that all the electronics work, and that I am capable of getting them all to talk to each other etc. Further, I haven't flown a custom made quad-copter before, so it might be wise to brush up on how they fly in a more simple and lower stakes environment. Good news: drones are just electronics held out on sticks, so I went and found some wooden dowel in my backyard and designed and 3d printed the components in an afternoon.

Quad testing

After some PID tuning, and ironing out hardware problems, the quad was flying great. After a bit of flying practise I went straight to experimentation to see what mechanical perculiaraties the flight controller could overcome. I swapped out one of the set of four motors for another one of a different kv rating. That flew fine. I swapped out propellers one at a time to see if that made a difference. Nope. This video shown below is proof that at least within reason the drone flies perfectly well with a mixture of different motors, props etc.

Stay tuned

This project is ongoing and is my main focus at the moment. I am still doing detailed design of the airframe and will start printing soon. I hope to put together a nicely edited video that will make this easier to digest. I hope to:

  • Once this version is flying, create my own flying wing design (this one was borrowed from the internet) which is tuned to work with more suitable electronics. i.e. optimise the design for efficiency not just prove it works.
  • Mix and match motor pairs, so the cruise motors are more efficient while the VTOL motors are small and light. I have proven within reason the FC can control minor differences but I would prefer to have ~8-9inch props on 1300kv motors for cruise and 5inch props on ~2300kv ish motors for VTOL. That may involve custom code but I'm interested to find out.
